What happens in the days after a car accident can shape everything — including how much you recover. Here is what you need to know before you say a word to anyone.

A Scenario Most People Know

You are stopped at a red light. Someone rear-ends you. The impact is jarring — your neck snaps forward, your hands grip the wheel. In the moment, you feel shaken but think you are okay. You exchange information, file a police report, and drive home. By the next morning, an insurance adjuster has already called asking how you are feeling and whether you would like to resolve things quickly.

That call is not a courtesy. It is a strategy. Insurance adjusters are trained to make early contact — before you have seen a doctor, before you understand the full extent of your injuries, and before you have spoken with an attorney. The goal is to get you to accept a settlement while your injuries are still invisible, your bills are still unpaid, and your rights are still unexercised. A quick offer can feel like relief. Often, it is the opposite.

"Whiplash, soft tissue damage, and concussions frequently do not show up for 24 to 72 hours. Signing early closes the door on compensation you may genuinely need."

Georgia law gives you two years to file a personal injury claim — but waiting is not the answer either. Evidence disappears. Witnesses forget. Medical records need to be preserved and properly documented from the start. The right move is to see a doctor immediately, say as little as possible to the insurer, and get a clear picture of what your case is actually worth before making any decisions. You are entitled to compensation for medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, and long-term impact on your life. Most people settle for a fraction of that because they did not know what to ask for.

The Bottom Line

If you or someone you know has been in a car accident — even one that seemed minor at the time — do not wait to get clarity. Do not give a recorded statement to an adjuster. Do not sign anything before speaking with an attorney. And do not assume that a quick settlement reflects what your case is actually worth.
